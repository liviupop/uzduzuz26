The default treatment of cultural heritage in much of Europe is curatorial. An object or a practice is identified, documented, classified, and then placed in a setting, physical or virtual, where it can be observed without being touched. This treatment has its uses. It also has a cost: the heritage so treated stops being a living thing and becomes a relic. The skill that produced it does not survive its placement in the display case.

We work with heritage in a different mode. The intangible heritage we care about, folk astronomy, traditional crafts, regional cuisines, vernacular architecture, oral literature, is interesting to us not as a subject of preservation but as a body of techniques that still know how to do things. A traditional joinery method, taught hand to hand, still produces a chair. A regional recipe, cooked in the kitchen where it was learned, still feeds a family. A folk astronomical name for a constellation still helps the person who knows it to find their way at night.

Our approach is therefore not to put these techniques behind glass, but to put them back into circulation. We bring practitioners and learners into the same room. We design projects in which heritage is the working material, not the subject of the slideshow. The aim is not nostalgia; it is the recognition that traditions, like languages, are kept alive by being used, and that an unused tradition will become a dead one within a generation.

This work involves a careful negotiation. Heritage that is over-romanticised becomes folklore in the worst sense, a tourist product detached from the conditions that made it meaningful. Heritage that is over-modernised loses the texture that made it worth caring about in the first place. We try to walk between the two failures, sometimes successfully, sometimes not. The negotiation has to be redone for each project, because each tradition has its own grain.

There is a quiet political content to this work, which connects it to several earlier principles. Living heritage gives a community a way of locating itself in time that does not depend on the cycle of consumption. It is an antidote to the perpetual present in which the digital economy would prefer that we live. The communities that have access to their own heritage, in the active sense, are more difficult to disorient.

To work with the past in this way is, paradoxically, to work in service of the future. The communities we form, the techniques we keep alive, the children we teach are all going to outlast us. That long horizon is itself a principle.
