The contemporary information environment is, by structure rather than by accident, hostile to slow thought. The platforms we read on are designed to maximise the time we spend on them; the tools we use to write are increasingly designed to predict what we are about to say; the news cycle has become a continuous shower of stimulus rather than a periodic delivery of considered material. The cumulative effect is a state that is best described as disorientation, in the literal sense of having lost one's bearings.

A disoriented population is, among other things, easier to manipulate. This is not a conspiracy theory; it is a description of what is for sale, openly, in the advertising and influence markets. The capacity to think slowly is a civic resource, and like any civic resource it can be depleted. We work, modestly, against that depletion.

Our methods are not heroic. We make work that takes a long time to read. We organise events that ask people to be in a room, with each other, for several hours. We resist the instinct to summarise our positions in slogans. We write essays on a website that does not have a recommendation algorithm. None of this is dramatic. It is the cumulative effect we are after, not any single intervention.

The principle has a defensive aspect and a constructive aspect.

The defensive aspect is the recognition that disorientation is being produced industrially, and that the production has consequences. We work in cultural and educational settings, where the consequences are most visible: a generation that has lost the habit of sustained attention struggles to learn complex things, including the complex things that democracy requires of its citizens.

The constructive aspect is more interesting. The capacity for orientation, once lost, can be rebuilt. The conditions for rebuilding it are, fortunately, the same conditions that produce most of the cultural goods we care about: time, community, careful work, the patience to be wrong, and the willingness to stay with a question. The principles we have listed in this manifesto, taken together, describe an organisation that tries to provide those conditions, project by project, year by year.

The list is now finished, but the work it describes is not. Each project we run is a small test of whether these eleven principles, held together, can produce something worth caring about. Some tests succeed; some fail in instructive ways. We continue.
