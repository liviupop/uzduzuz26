Critical technology is the slower cousin of digital transformation. The two share a vocabulary; they do not share a horizon. Digital transformation, as the term is used by consultancies, is interested in the speed at which an organisation can adopt new tools. Critical technology is interested in what those tools have done, are doing, and might do, to the people who use them and to the people they are used on.

The distinction matters because we live in a moment when very large decisions about how technology should be designed are being made very quickly, often by very few people. The question of who decides, on whose behalf, with what consequence, is not a question that can be answered by adoption metrics. It requires the older intellectual equipment that lives in philosophy departments, anthropology departments, civic associations, and quiet workshops like ours.

We try to do three things in this register.

First, we work with technology as practitioners, not as commentators. We use the tools, including the most recent ones, in our own projects. Commentary at a distance is too easy and too unreliable; the only commentary worth offering is the kind that comes from having tried.

Second, we slow down at the points where speed is being demanded. When everyone else in the room is excited about a new model or a new platform, we have learned to be the ones asking what kind of work it makes harder, what kind of relationships it inserts itself into, and who is paying the cost of its training data.

Third, we name the political content of technical decisions. There is no neutral algorithm; there is only an algorithm whose politics has been hidden well enough that nobody is asking. We try to do the asking, in our writing, in our workshops, and in the projects we choose to take on.

This is unfashionable work. The reward for doing it is not large. The risk of not doing it, however, is the gradual erasure of intellectual habits that took several centuries to develop and that will not regenerate spontaneously once they are gone. We accept the unfashionable label.

A technology that has been examined critically can be put to use without fear. The tools that emerge from this examination are, in fact, often better suited to the long forms of cultural work we care about. That brings us to the principle that follows.
