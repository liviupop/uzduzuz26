Most of what is called education today is, in practice, training for a specific function. The student learns how to operate a piece of software, how to write a particular kind of report, how to pass a specific examination. Training is not nothing; it is, however, not enough. A person who has only been trained will struggle the first time the function changes, which in our economy is approximately every five years.

We work in a different tradition. Inspired by Comenius, Friedrich Froebel, Maria Montessori, Janusz Korczak and other perhaps less famous thinkers who agreed on a simple principle: a human being is not a vessel to be filled with information, but a whole organism with a body, a memory, a hand, a moral sense, an aesthetic faculty, and a set of unanswered questions. Education that addresses only the cognitive faculty, leaving the others uncultivated, produces adults who feel competent and unsatisfied at the same time.

We try, in our projects, to engage as many faculties as we can in the same activity. A workshop on traditional craft asks the hand to learn what the eye has seen; a seminar on contemporary politics asks the moral sense to test what the reason has accepted; an astronomy camp asks the imagination to extend what the telescope has shown. None of this is innovation. It is what good teachers have always done. Our contribution is to keep doing it in a moment when the dominant model of education would prefer that we did not.

The principle applies to adults as much as to children. Adult education is often treated as a remedial activity, a way to fix what was missed earlier; we treat it as a continuation of the same lifelong work of formation. The retiree who comes to a workshop on regional cuisine and the schoolchild who comes to a workshop on retrofuturism are, in some important sense, doing the same thing.

Education of this sort is, by its nature, a civic activity. It assumes that the person being formed will eventually take their place in a community and that they will be expected to think for themselves there. The next principle takes up that consequence directly.
