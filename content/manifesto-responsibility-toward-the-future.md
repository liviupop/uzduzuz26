We are the ancestors of the people who will inherit our decisions. The thought is sufficiently sobering to keep us, on most days, from grandstanding. It is not a dramatic claim; every generation has been the ancestor of the next. What makes the present moment unusual is the speed at which our decisions, taken now, will close off options for the people who come later. The climate is the obvious example; the cultural and institutional infrastructure is the less obvious one.

Responsibility toward the future is not the same as anxiety about the future. The anxious organisation freezes; it cannot act, because every action seems to risk a catastrophe. The responsible organisation acts deliberately, accepting that imperfect action now is preferable to perfect deliberation forever, while keeping the long horizon in view. We try to work in the second register.

Practically, this means three things.

First, the projects we run have to be defensible at a distance of decades, not only at a distance of months. If the test were only short-term, we could justify almost anything; the longer test is harder. We sometimes refuse work that would pass the short-term test and fail the long one. The refusal is not always elegant.

Second, the organisations we form have to be sustainable in the boring institutional sense: financially honest, administratively careful, not over-extended. An organisation that collapses, however brilliantly, has failed its future obligations. We try not to be brilliant in that way.

Third, the people we work with, especially the young ones, are not instruments of our projects. They are the people who will have to deal with the consequences of those projects. We try to treat them accordingly, which means, among other things, not asking them to perform enthusiasm they do not feel and not promising them outcomes we cannot deliver.

The horizon of responsibility we are describing is generational. Generational thinking is countercultural in a moment that prefers quarterly thinking. We notice this and continue anyway. Most of the work that has been worth doing, in any era, has been done at a tempo that did not match the dominant one of its time.

The opposite of generational thinking is not, however, short-termism in the abstract. It is something more specific: the deliberate disorientation that the contemporary information environment now produces at scale. The final principle is our response to that disorientation.
