Most contemporary technology is designed to attract attention. The notification, the autoplaying video, the prompt to engage: these are not accidents of interface design but the operating principles of a particular industry. We notice them because they have started to colonise rooms that used to be quiet, including the rooms where we work.

There is an older idea, articulated in the late nineteen-nineties at Xerox PARC, of technology that recedes. Calm technology, as the original phrase has it, sits at the periphery of attention. It tells you what you need to know without insisting that you stop and look. A teakettle that whistles when the water boils is, in this sense, a more sophisticated piece of design than most software produced today: it interrupts you only when interruption is justified, and it is silent the rest of the time.

We try to keep this idea visible in our work. The artificial intelligence we use, when we use it, sits in our toolbox alongside the pencil and the spreadsheet. We do not stage it as a marvel; we ask it to do something and then we close the window. The educational materials we produce do not chase engagement metrics. The events we organise are not livestreamed for an algorithmic audience. The website you are reading is, by deliberate choice, slow to load nothing and quick to show you what you came for.

This is not a refusal of technology. It is a posture toward it: we keep the relationship right-sized. The instrument serves the work; the work does not serve the instrument. When the relationship inverts, as it sometimes does, we notice and we step back.

The discipline of staying quiet about technology is not the same as being indifferent to it. It is, in fact, a precondition for the kind of education we believe in: education that forms the whole person rather than training a partial one. That is the next principle.
