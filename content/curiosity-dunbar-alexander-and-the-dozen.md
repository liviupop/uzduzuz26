# Dunbar, Alexander, and the dozen

Why a dozen people, and not a hundred or three? Two unrelated traditions, an evolutionary anthropologist counting neocortex ratios and an architect sketching workshops, converge on the same number. This note is a long footnote to the [Who we are](who-we-are) page.

## The 1:3 scaling ratio (Zhou et al., 2005)

Robin Dunbar's research on primate social cognition identifies four concentric layers of relationships a human being can sustain. Zhou and colleagues showed that each layer is roughly three times the size of the one inside it. The fit is empirical, observed across cultures and across very different kinds of grouping: hunter-gatherer bands, military squads to companies, monastic communities, well-functioning research labs.

The brain does not scale relationships smoothly; it batches them into nested layers, each demanding roughly one-third the cognitive intensity of the one within it. The sympathy group, around fifteen people, is the layer at which a small organisation can still be coherent: everyone knows everyone by name, by trade, by family situation. Beyond it, the texture changes.

## The self-governing workshop (Alexander, 1977)

Christopher Alexander's *A Pattern Language* is, among other things, an architect's attempt to articulate the conditions under which human institutions stay healthy. Pattern 80, *Self-Governing Workshops and Offices*, identifies a working group of five to twenty people as the unit at which work can be done without an internal hierarchy: the group is small enough that everyone in it knows the others well enough to coordinate by conversation rather than by command.

Alexander wrote in 1977; Dunbar's quantitative work would not appear until the early 1990s. Two independent lineages, one drawn from craft and observation, the other from the comparative anatomy of primate brains, converge on the same range. We notice this and continue.

## Why this matters for us

uzinaduzina was named before either of us had read Dunbar or Alexander carefully. The name was a working hypothesis: that a dozen committed people, each carrying their own research and projects, could produce more together than a single larger institution would, while remaining humane enough to actually live inside. The literature, found later, turned out to support the intuition.

The practical consequence for the organisation is that we do not grow by hiring. We scale by partnership: through European consortia, through commissioned collaborators, through long-running relationships with sister organisations across the continent. The core group stays small on purpose. Below twelve, we lose the diversity of competences that complex projects need; above fifteen, we lose the ability to know one another well enough to coordinate without overhead. The name is, in effect, a permanent reminder.
