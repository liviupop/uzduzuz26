A curious organisation is one that protects the right of its members to follow a question without knowing the answer in advance. This sounds modest until you try to do it inside the modern grant economy, where every application requires that the outcomes be specified before the work has begun. We have learned to write those applications honestly while preserving, inside the projects themselves, room for genuine surprise.

Curiosity is not the same as restlessness. A restless organisation chases the new because the new is exciting; it abandons last year's interests as soon as this year's arrive. A curious organisation stays with a question long enough to find out what is actually behind it, which is often something very different from what was visible at the start. The two postures look similar from the outside and are nearly opposite from the inside.

Experiment is the other half of the principle. By experiment we mean a project undertaken deliberately to test a hypothesis, with the understanding that the test might fail. Some of the projects we have run are experiments that worked, in the sense that the hypothesis was confirmed and the project produced a deliverable. Others are experiments that taught us something else: that the hypothesis was wrong, that the question was badly framed, that the partners we had assembled were not the right ones. We count the latter as successes too, by a different measure. The information they generated has shaped what we do next.

We try to be honest about which kind of project a given activity is. A workshop with twenty children and a clear pedagogical goal is not an experiment; it is an application of methods we already know. A new format involving four organisations from three countries and a question none of us have asked before is an experiment, and we say so. The vocabulary matters because it sets expectations for everyone involved.

Experiment, in our usage, is also distinct from innovation. Innovation is a word from the start-up vocabulary; it carries connotations of disruption, growth, and competitive advantage. Experiment is a word from the laboratory and from the workshop; it carries connotations of patience, repetition, and the willingness to be wrong. We prefer the older word.

The willingness to experiment, sustained over years, leaves an organisation with a particular relationship to its own tools. The most consequential of those tools, in our period, is technology. We have a specific posture toward it, which is the next principle.
