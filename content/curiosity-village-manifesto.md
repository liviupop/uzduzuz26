A general guide for digital nomads and remote workers who want to embrace the rural lifestyle. Eleven principles, signed in 2020 by twenty-something people who recognised themselves in them. First published on the 2020-era uzinaduzina website and kept here as a standalone text of its own.

This is a separate document from the current organisational [fundamentals](fundamentals). The 2020 manifesto reads on its own terms, with its own internal sequence of eleven principles, and is especially close to the [Living heritage](manifesto-living-heritage) line of work it foreshadowed.

## § 1. With roots in traditions and head in the clouds

Tradition means continuity. Tradition means flow. You have no roots, you have no fruits. But the internet surrounds us everywhere and our fruits are digital. Remember, the future is here, it's just not evenly spread everywhere. You yourself can be the one to bring the future to the more remote places.

*Example:* Morkin House, Serbia.

## § 2. Respect the community and the lifestyle of neighbors

You may be just a visitor or a commuter, but the people around you have been living here, in the community, for a long time. There is a good chance that the pace of life in the village is different from what you are used to, and the customs are certainly different. It is possible to discover complex relationships and long and complicated stories in the community, with good and bad. Before you judge, try to understand what is happening around you and do everything you can to adapt a little.

*Example:* Creative Traditions.

## § 3. Support the community

Some of the communities you visit may be wealthy, but others will lack many of the opportunities you are used to in the city. Education, economic life, and healthcare may seem minimal, but you could be the one planting seeds with long-term impact. Share generously what you know best professionally, seek out young, bright minds who want to learn, and help them develop the skills you are familiar with. Young generations are the future, do your best to help them grow.

*Example:* Rubik's Cube.

## § 4. Be a role model for local youth

As someone who has traveled extensively and has been successful in the urban space, you will likely be perceived as someone who has had access to a life that some young people are starting to dream of. Be generous and help them, tell them your story and how you came to choose that lifestyle. Teach them how to use the resources available online, speak to them in English, and encourage them to spend some time investing in their personal development. If you are open, little by little the community will open its arms to welcome you, and your impact will grow.

*Example:* Piscu School.

## § 5. Be disruptive, but not toxic

As a foreigner, you stir up trouble and change things around you from the moment you arrive in a community. You may like to be discreet, but in small communities, it's quite difficult. Even if your neighbors don't show it to you, you arouse their curiosity and they will look for opportunities to chat. Your impact can be small or large, but it's good to be aware of it. Imagine your village as a delicate ecosystem and try to assess what effects your choices will have.

## § 6. Take care of the plants and animals around you

Rural spaces are immersed in nature. Whether you are on a mountaintop or by the sea, your gaze will fall on green expanses and there is a good chance that birds will pass by you. Your neighbors may have seeds that no one has known about for a long time or share their milk or eggs with you. You are closer than ever to the first link in the consumption chain. Enjoy it and respect the environment that offers you these goodies. Try to grow something yourself, but also listen to the advice of those who know the land better.

*Example:* EcoRuralis, Free Seeds.

## § 7. Cherish vernacular architecture

Sometimes bizarre and highly personal, sometimes kitschy and daring, the architecture you will find is a mix of archaic forms and building materials with modern and contemporary versions misinterpreted. You will discover buildings that are completely missing from the city: dog kennels, stables, barns and shepherds' shelters. The fences will be made of metal, wood, plastic, concrete and all sorts of other materials. But if you look carefully, you are likely to find little gems that will melt your heart, fragments of a past long gone and forgotten in the cities. If you have the opportunity and decide to build something yourself, be inspired by the unique forms around you, ask about materials and local craftsmen and involve them in your project.

*Examples:* Architectural guides for country buildings, Raum Architecture.

## § 8. The village is the place where hard work is done

In the village, there are few of the comforts or entertainments you were used to. It is a good opportunity to focus on projects or decisions that you have postponed for a more favorable moment or quieter days. These are quieter days, so it is time to work harder. So do your neighbors, sometimes with less profit than you. Do the same and leave the excuses, banish laziness. With a little determination, you could exchange physical exercise for a daily dose of agricultural work.

## § 9. Invite townspeople to visit

Maybe there is a town nearby where some of your friends live. Ask them to visit you, you could spend a nice afternoon together in the fresh air. Show them around, introduce them to your acquaintances in the village, go berry picking. You may find that many of them yearn for what you are experiencing and have very specific questions about how you have adapted to the new context. Since you have already spent a few weeks or months in the village, you can show them the most beautiful parts; both the locals and your friends will be grateful to you, happy to discover new things. Your friends' visit could boost the morale of the villagers, a sign that you have been a good ambassador, another reason to be appreciated by the community.

*Example:* Transylvanian Brunch.

## § 10. Support local producers

From fresh vegetables to honey, homemade pickles and jams or sheep's cheeses and traditional recipes that you can only find here, this is your chance to eat and live like the villagers. Although they may not be labeled as such, most of the products are organic and almost certainly very tasty. To consume locally, you will probably have to adapt your old habits a little, but the reward will be great not only for you, but also for the local producers. If you think some of them deserve to have access to a larger market, help them: a label made by a professional, a small website and a few marketing tricks could work wonders and have a huge impact on the producers you appreciate. You may not know it, but bartering in the rural world is still a reality, one that could prove inviting.

*Example:* Huedin Market / Sustainable Kalotaszeg Tarnat Association.

## § 11. Party with the street musicians

Maybe you miss the nightlife or bars, a 3D movie or experimental concerts. Ask your favorite indie artists if they would like to play an outdoor unplugged concert. Or find a birthday that would be the perfect occasion for an electro party in the woods. Artists are very mobile and might appreciate a change of scenery. While your tastes may not overlap with those of most villagers, the few occasions when you hear traditional local bands could be a breath of fresh air. You may not have many entertainment options around you, but that doesn't mean you can't create your own.

*Examples:* Days of the North, Duruşa Summer Hills.
