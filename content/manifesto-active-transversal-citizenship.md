Citizenship, in the standard account, is something you exercise on a few specific occasions: when you vote, when you attend a town hall, when you write to your representative. The rest of the time you are off duty. We think this account is too thin. The civic muscle, like any other, weakens when it is used only at intervals. By the time the next election arrives, it is no longer in shape to do what is being asked of it.

We work with a different account. Citizenship, in our usage, is a habit that infiltrates ordinary activities. It shows up when a group of people decides together how to use a shared room, when an organisation chooses one supplier over another for reasons that include but exceed price, when a workshop is structured so that every voice in it carries weight. None of these moments looks like politics in the formal sense; all of them are, nonetheless, the practice from which formal politics has to be built.

The word *transversal* is doing real work here. We mean two things by it. First, that civic practice cuts across disciplines: it shows up in arts projects, in scientific projects, in heritage projects, not only in projects that are explicitly civic. Second, that civic practice cuts across professions and ages: a fourteen-year-old in a workshop and a seventy-year-old in the same workshop are doing the same kind of thing when they speak up, listen, change their minds.

This is why we are so reluctant to design projects that are only about one thing. A project that is only about civic education will produce, at best, civic information. A project that is about astronomy or about woodworking or about contemporary art, but that has been structured so that civic practice is unavoidable inside it, will produce something more durable: people who have used the muscle.

There is an obvious risk, which is that a sufficiently capacious idea of citizenship can become a synonym for any kind of social participation, at which point it loses its meaning. We try to keep the term honest by anchoring it explicitly in European democratic culture, which has both a tradition and a content. That commitment is the next principle.
