The self-governing workshop

Christopher Alexander's Pattern 80 in A Pattern Language (1977) describes a working group small enough that all members know each other, share a common space, and can govern themselves without an internal hierarchy. The number he proposes overlaps almost exactly with Dunbar's sympathy group.

The pattern

Alexander observed that workplaces grow rotten beyond a certain size. Once a workshop becomes large enough that the people in it no longer know each other by name, by trade, by family situation, the texture of work changes. Hierarchies appear; coordination costs rise; the worker begins to feel like a cog rather than a participant.

"No one enjoys his work if he is a cog in a machine. […] Encourage the formation of self-governing workshops and offices of 5 to 20 workers. Make each group autonomous, with respect to its own internal organisation, its methods, its hours, its policies."

Pattern 80, Self-Governing Workshops and Offices. The range Alexander gives, 5 to 20, brackets the second Dunbar layer almost exactly. The pattern was published in 1977; Dunbar's quantitative work on social cognition would not appear until the early 1990s. Two independent traditions, an architect drawing on craft and observation, an evolutionary anthropologist drawing on neocortex measurements, converge on the same number.

A connected pattern, Pattern 148: Small Work Groups, refines the observation: even within a larger institution, work happens best in clusters of around half a dozen people who share the same room and the same daily rhythm.

Reading: Alexander, Ishikawa & Silverstein, A Pattern Language, Oxford University Press, 1977. Patterns 80 and 148.
