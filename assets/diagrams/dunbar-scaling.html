The 1:3 scaling ratio

Zhou and colleagues showed in 2005 that each Dunbar layer is roughly three times the size of the one inside it. The ratio is empirical, observed across many human social structures, and remarkably stable.

The relation

Across the four layers, each successive grouping contains approximately three times the members of the one within it. Written as a recurrence:

L(n+1) ≈ 3 · L(n)

Starting from the support clique of about five people, the sequence becomes: 5, 15, 45 (observed: 50), 135 (observed: 150). The fit is not perfect, but the ratio holds across cultures and across very different kinds of human grouping: hunter-gatherer bands, military squads to companies, monastic communities, well-functioning research labs.

Zhou and colleagues (2005) interpret this as evidence that human social cognition has a discrete hierarchical structure rather than a continuous one. The brain does not scale relationships smoothly; it batches them into nested layers, each demanding roughly one-third the cognitive intensity of the one within it.

After Zhou, Sornette, Hill & Dunbar, "Discrete hierarchical organization of social group sizes", Proceedings of the Royal Society B, 272(1561), 2005.
